_**NGC 4490: Cocoon Galaxy**. Taken using the Celestron C14 telescopes at UCL Observatory_
NGC 4490 is a barred-spiral galaxy, located roughly 25 million light-years from Earth and spanning about 45,000 light-years in diameter. It forms an interacting pair with its smaller companion NGC 4485; together they comprise the system Arp 269. Their gravitational encounter triggered strong star-formation activity: NGC 4490 currently forms stars at a rate of about 4.7 solar masses per year, and has maintained roughly that rate for ~2 billion years. Dense clouds of gas and dust, dominate the galaxy, and bright H II regions indicate star-forming regions, and a long stream of stars, gas and dust connects the two galaxies. NGC 4490 has also hosted at least two supernovae, and shows evidence for a double-nucleus: one visible, the other obscured by dust and detected at non-optical wavelengths, hinting at a complex merger/interaction history.
